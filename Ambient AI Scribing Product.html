How ambient AI is quietly solving medicine's most overlooked crisis — one conversation at a time.

The Finding

A randomized trial in JAMA returned something unexpected to doctors.

In 2025, researchers published a randomized controlled trial in JAMA — medicine's most-read journal. They measured a single, precise thing: how much time physicians spend on clinical documentation. Then they gave half of those physicians an AI that listened to their conversations and wrote the notes automatically.

The results were unambiguous. Across an 8-hour clinical day, physicians using ambient AI reclaimed meaningful hours of their time — not at the cost of quality, but because the tool was doing the capturing while they were doing the caring.

But how much time? And for which doctors? The honest answer is: it depends. And that nuance is exactly what makes the findings more interesting, not less.

The Problem

A Tuesday at 9am — from both sides of the stethoscope.

Dr. Priya is logging into the EMR before the door fully opens. The patient — a 58-year-old man, third visit this month — settles into the chair. He starts talking. She types while he speaks, abbreviating, trusting her memory for the details she can't capture fast enough.

Physical examination. She stands, moves to the patient. The keyboard goes quiet. She washes her hands. Returns to the desk. The note sits unfinished. Four minutes before the next patient.

And across the desk, the patient watches a physician doing everything right — and wonders, quietly, whether she's really listening.

"This is not a story about a doctor cutting corners. It is a story about two people trying their best inside a system that divides their attention in half."

The Shift

Same Tuesday. Same patient. A small indicator on a tablet: Listening.

Dr. Priya sits down. Faces the patient fully. He starts talking. She asks questions she wouldn't have thought of while typing. He notices something — she's making eye contact. He relaxes.

Physical examination flows without interruption. She washes her hands. The patient leaves at 9:17. She glances at her tablet: a structured clinical note — history, findings, assessment, plan — already drafted. She reads it, adjusts two phrases, and signs.

9:19am. Two minutes ahead of schedule.

Ambient AI listens to the conversation, maps it to clinical structure in real time, and surfaces a reviewable draft note. No keyboard during the encounter. No memory tax. The physician reviews, edits, and signs — and moves forward.

The Evidence

What the JAMA trial actually found — and why the honest answer is more powerful.

Not every physician carries the same documentation burden. A primary care doctor navigating 20-25 patients a day sits at the high end; a specialist with fewer but longer encounters sits somewhere different. The JAMA study acknowledged this — and that's what makes the result credible.

Across physicians, the reduction in documentation time as a share of their working day was significant. When you translate that into real weekly hours — the kind that pile up and quietly erode a career — the number becomes visceral.

Three to seven hours a week sounds like a number. But let's be concrete: that's the difference between leaving the hospital at 5:30 and staying until 7. Between having dinner with your family and reheating it alone.

"This isn't a productivity metric. It's a measure of what medicine was supposed to feel like."

The #1 cited cause: EHR burden. This is not a personal failure. It is a design problem — and design problems have design solutions.

Health systems and physicians are on the same side of this. Leaders want their physicians present, sustainable, and practising at the top of their licence. Physicians want to give their patients the full attention they came for. The EMR, as currently designed, works against both — not through malice, but through accumulated complexity that nobody stopped to question.

Ambient AI is not a workaround. It is a structural response to a structural problem, now validated at the highest level of medical evidence.

What's Next

The problem ambient scribing doesn't fully solve.

Here's something that surprises people: even though writing notes is what feels most painful, navigating the EMR is where a larger share of time quietly disappears. Reviewing a patient's record before an encounter — scrolling through previous notes, hunting for a lab trend, piecing together a medication history — is a cognitive tax the system levies before the patient even walks in.

Critical patterns — a rising creatinine trend across six visits, a drug interaction buried three tabs deep — require the physician to do the surfacing work themselves. The information exists. The burden of finding it is theirs.

This is where we are building next. A pre-visit summary that brings what matters to the surface — recent changes, flagged values, care gaps — before the patient enters the room. Not another screen to manage. A smarter version of the one that's already there.

We are genuinely excited about this. If the ambient scribe gave physicians their voice back in the room, intelligent navigation gives them their memory back before it. The time savings compound. And the case for what medicine can feel like gets a little stronger every time.
